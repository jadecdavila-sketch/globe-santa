A note from design

Designing Globe Santa online.

What we're building — and what we're protecting.

For seventy years, Globe Santa has run on a beautifully simple loop. Families write letters. The team reads every one. Gifts arrive before Christmas. And excerpts from those letters fund the next year through the Globe's editorial pages.

The digital application we're building has to extend that program, not replace it. The risk in any modernization like this one is that the things that make Globe Santa work — voice, dignity, editorial care — get quietly hollowed out in the name of efficiency. We see an opportunity to use AI thoughtfully here: so that more families can apply, in more languages, with less friction, while what reaches Tammy and the editors is more of the family's authentic voice, not less.

The principles below are the design commitments that make that opportunity real. They are how we'll decide the small questions, and how we'll defend the big ones.

· · ·

What we believe

Six commitments.

  1. The letter is the program.

    The letter does three jobs at once: it qualifies the family operationally, it becomes the editorial content that funds next year's giving, and it is an act of testimony — the family being heard. Every design decision is tested against all three.

  2. Capture, don't curate. Translate, don't ventriloquize.

    AI lowers the barriers to entry — literacy, language, the "I don't know how to put this into words." It never sits between the family and how they sound. The voice that reaches Tammy is the family's voice.

  3. Voice preservation is non-negotiable.

    Original audio or text is always preserved as the source of record. AI may produce additional artifacts — transcription, translation, structured triage data — but never replaces what the family actually said. Excerpts published in The Globe draw from the original or a faithful translation, never AI-polished prose. Authorship stays with the family.

  4. AI augments human work; humans always decide.

    Every AI surface in the team's review tool is opinionated but reversible. The system suggests categories, surfaces relevant context, flags anomalies for review. Tammy and the editors accept, reject, override, or ignore. Nothing changes silently.

  5. Soft, never gated. Warm, never alarming.

    The form is a conversation, not a turnstile. Disabled buttons with gentle helper text guide families through what's needed. No character minimums on the letter, no error-state red, no email-verification gates stacked against families who most need help. Friction must justify itself against real risks for this program — not borrowed from generic best-practice templates.

  6. Equity is a design requirement, not a phase 2.

    Multi-language input from day one, voice for families uncomfortable typing, mobile-first for those who are mobile-only. Globe Santa already runs a Spanish-language phone line. The digital application should continue that promise visibly, in every language Greater Boston families speak.

Specifically

What AI touches — and what it doesn't.

Principles only matter if you can point to where they live. Below is every place AI touches a family's application or a team member's review: what it does, why, and what we've expressly designed it not to do. Eight surfaces in total — four on the family side, four on the team side. All are demonstrated in the companion prototypes.

For families · four surfaces
Voice transcription Family-facing
Does Converts spoken audio in the family's chosen language into written text the family can review and edit before submitting.
Why Removes the literacy barrier. A parent who can't easily type — especially in English — can still apply in their own words.
Doesn't Change the words. Add content. Decide what's "good enough" or "long enough." The original audio is preserved as the source of record.
Language auto-detection Family-facing
Does Identifies the language the family is speaking or writing, and shows what was detected as a small visible chip.
Why Removes the upfront "select your language" decision so the form welcomes families in whatever language they bring.
Doesn't Override the family's choice. Translate silently. The picker remains visible and tappable for correction at any time.
Translation for the team Family-facing
Does Produces an English translation of letters submitted in other languages, alongside the preserved original.
Why Lets the editorial team read every letter without requiring families to write in English they may not be comfortable using.
Doesn't Replace the original. Polish or rewrite. Smooth out cultural or personal voice. Excerpts published in The Globe draw from the original or this faithful translation, with attribution — never AI-rewritten prose.
Soft completeness prompts Family-facing
Does After a letter is captured, may ask one or two operational questions if specific data is missing — for example, children's interests for gift selection.
Why Helps families provide enough information for the program to serve them well, without imposing word counts or character minimums.
Doesn't Ask editorial questions ("tell us more about your hardship"). Gate submission. Set a minimum length. Each prompt is optional and skippable.
For the team · four surfaces
Operational checks Team-facing
Does Runs background verification of required fields, USPS-validated address, delivery-area eligibility, and prior-year participation. Presents results as a checklist.
Why Removes manual cross-referencing per application so Tammy and the team spend their time on the letter, not on lookups.
Doesn't Make eligibility decisions. Reject applications. Anything that can't be auto-confirmed is surfaced for human review, never resolved silently.
Letter annotations Team-facing
Does Highlights spans of the letter where context may be relevant — job loss mentioned, an additional child outside the age range, a recent move. Reasoning revealed on hover.
Why Surfaces signal Tammy might want to know without forcing her to find it. Speeds reading without replacing it.
Doesn't Modify the letter. Hide content. Make claims about the family. Annotations are pure overlay on top of unchanged text.
Category suggestions Team-facing
Does Proposes editorial categories drawn from Tammy's existing taxonomy, with confidence levels, presented as accept/reject pills.
Why Turns blank-page categorization into a faster reviewing task — accept what's right, dismiss what isn't, add anything missed.
Doesn't Apply categories without explicit human approval. Override the team's taxonomy. Confidence levels are context, not authority.
Editor flag rationale Team-facing
Does Notes whether a letter has elements that often resonate in fundraising stories, with the AI's reasoning visible above the toggle.
Why Surfaces editorial-pipeline candidates so flags reach the editorial team faster and more consistently.
Doesn't Flag without an explicit human toggle. Decide what runs in the paper. The toggle is off by default; the AI's case must convince a human first.

The trade we're proposing

What we're optimizing for, and against.

For

  • Warmth and dignity at every touchpoint
  • Voice — preserved, never replaced
  • Access for the families most often left out
  • Editorial integrity in fundraising stories
  • Tammy's time on what only humans can do

Against

  • Friction stacked on the most vulnerable
  • AI doing invisible work on families' words
  • "Modernization" that quietly changes the program
  • Efficiency gains paid for in trust
  • Defaults that work for some, not all
· · ·

Decisions still needed

Open questions.

These are the calls that the design needs from the program — Foundation, editorial, legal, and operational together. The prototypes can carry placeholders until they're answered.

  1. Editorial disclosure. When fundraising story excerpts come from translated text, what's the right disclosure? "Translated from Spanish" alongside the quote? In a footer note?
  2. Voice recording privacy. Where do voice recordings live? How long are they retained, and who has access — particularly when the recording mentions minors?
  3. The editorial taxonomy. What categories does Tammy use today? The AI suggestions should reflect her real working vocabulary, not generic guesses.
  4. The DTA verification gate. What does the family see and feel during the wait between submission and verification? What does our communication say?
  5. Multilingual scope at launch. Which languages ship with v1? Spanish at minimum; Haitian Creole, Portuguese, Vietnamese, Cantonese all candidates.
  6. The soft-launch audience. How are the 500–1,000 pilot families chosen, and what specifically are we measuring before scaling to the full program?
